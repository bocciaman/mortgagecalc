Property taxes are one of the most overlooked costs in home buying, largely because they vary so dramatically by location that a "typical" number is almost meaningless. Understanding how they're calculated — and how they fold into your monthly payment — can prevent a significant budgeting surprise.

## How Property Taxes Are Calculated

Property taxes are generally calculated as a percentage of your home's **assessed value**, which may differ from its market value depending on your local assessment practices and timing. The formula is simple in concept:

**Annual Property Tax = Assessed Value × Local Tax Rate**

The tax rate (sometimes called the mill rate or millage rate) is set by your local government — county, city, school district, and sometimes additional special districts — and funds public services like schools, roads, emergency services, and libraries.

## Why Rates Vary So Much by Location

Effective property tax rates can range from under 0.3% of home value annually in some states to over 2% in others — a massive difference that has nothing to do with home price and everything to do with location. Two identical $400,000 homes in different states could have annual tax bills that differ by $6,000 or more.

This is one of the biggest reasons a "national average" mortgage payment estimate is almost useless for actual budgeting. Always look up the specific property tax rate for the exact location you're considering — county assessor websites typically publish current rates and often let you look up a specific property's tax history.

## How Property Taxes Fold Into Your Monthly Payment

Most lenders require property taxes to be paid through an **escrow account**, where 1/12th of your estimated annual tax bill is collected as part of your monthly mortgage payment, alongside principal, interest, and insurance (together known as PITI — see our full breakdown in [Understanding PITI](/articles/understanding-piti-mortgage-payment/)).

This means your "mortgage payment" is often significantly higher than just the principal and interest figure advertised by lenders or seen in online rate comparisons. Our [Mortgage Calculator](/mortgage-calculator/) includes a dedicated property tax field so you can see your full estimated payment, not just the principal and interest portion.

## What Happens If Your Assessment Changes

Property tax bills aren't fixed for the life of your loan. If your local government reassesses your home's value — common after a sale, renovation, or periodic county-wide reassessment — your tax bill (and therefore your monthly escrow payment) can increase. Lenders periodically review your escrow account and adjust your monthly payment if your actual tax bill differs from what was originally estimated.

This is an important detail many buyers miss: your principal and interest payment is fixed on a fixed-rate loan, but your *total* monthly payment can still increase over time due to rising property taxes or insurance premiums.

## Property Tax Exemptions Worth Researching

Many states and municipalities offer property tax exemptions or reductions that can meaningfully lower your bill:

- **Homestead exemptions** — reduce taxable value for owner-occupied primary residences in many states
- **Senior citizen exemptions** — additional reductions for qualifying homeowners over a certain age
- **Veteran exemptions** — reductions or exemptions for eligible veterans, varying by state
- **Disability exemptions** — available in many jurisdictions for qualifying homeowners

These programs require active application in most cases — they're rarely applied automatically. Check with your local county assessor's office after closing to see what you may qualify for.

## Factoring Property Taxes Into Your Home Search

Before falling in love with a specific neighborhood, research the property tax rate for that exact area — rates can vary meaningfully even between neighboring towns or school districts within the same county. A home with a lower purchase price but a high local tax rate can end up costing more monthly than a slightly pricier home in a lower-tax area.

Use our [Affordability Calculator](/affordability-calculator/) with realistic property tax estimates for your target area to avoid budgeting based on principal and interest alone.

## The Bottom Line

Property taxes are a permanent, recurring cost of homeownership that varies enormously by location and can change over time. Researching the actual local rate — not a national average — before you buy, and budgeting your full PITI payment rather than just principal and interest, protects you from one of the most common first-time buyer budgeting mistakes.

For general information on how property assessments work, the [Lincoln Institute of Land Policy's property tax database](https://www.lincolninst.edu/) provides comparative data across U.S. states and localities.

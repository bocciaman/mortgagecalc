Buying your first home is exciting, overwhelming, and — for most people — the single largest financial transaction they'll ever make. The process can feel like a maze of paperwork, jargon, and decisions, but it doesn't have to be confusing. Here's a clear, step-by-step roadmap to help you go from "thinking about it" to holding the keys.

## Step 1: Check Your Credit and Finances

Before you even start browsing listings, take an honest look at your financial picture. Lenders will scrutinize your credit score, debt levels, and income, so you should too.

Pull your free credit reports at [AnnualCreditReport.com](https://www.annualcreditreport.com) and check for errors. A higher credit score typically unlocks better interest rates — even a 0.5% difference in rate can save (or cost) you tens of thousands of dollars over a 30-year loan. If your score needs work, see our guide on [how to improve your credit score before applying for a mortgage](/articles/how-to-improve-credit-score-before-mortgage/).

## Step 2: Figure Out What You Can Actually Afford

It's tempting to look at homes based on what a lender says you're *approved* for, but approved and *comfortable* are two different numbers. Most financial advisors recommend the 28/36 rule: no more than 28% of your gross monthly income on housing costs, and no more than 36% on total debt.

Use our free [Affordability Calculator](/affordability-calculator/) to plug in your income, debts, and down payment to get a realistic price range before you fall in love with a house outside your budget.

## Step 3: Save for a Down Payment and Closing Costs

While 20% down is the traditional benchmark (and helps you avoid PMI — more on that below), many loan programs allow far less:

- **Conventional loans:** as low as 3% down for qualified buyers
- **FHA loans:** as low as 3.5% down
- **VA loans:** 0% down for eligible veterans and service members
- **USDA loans:** 0% down in eligible rural areas

Don't forget closing costs, which typically run 2–5% of the loan amount. Read our full breakdown in [Closing Costs Explained](/articles/closing-costs-explained/).

## Step 4: Get Pre-Approved, Not Just Pre-Qualified

Pre-qualification is a quick estimate based on self-reported numbers. Pre-approval involves an actual credit check and documentation review, resulting in a conditional commitment letter. Sellers take pre-approved buyers far more seriously — in competitive markets, an offer without pre-approval often won't even get a response.

Shop at least 3 lenders for pre-approval. Rates and fees vary more than most buyers expect, and a small difference in rate compounds significantly over time. Run different rate scenarios through our [Mortgage Calculator](/mortgage-calculator/) to see the real dollar impact.

## Step 5: Find a Real Estate Agent

A buyer's agent is typically paid by the seller's side of the transaction, meaning their guidance often costs you nothing directly. A good agent will help you understand local market conditions, write competitive offers, and navigate negotiations.

## Step 6: House Hunt with a Checklist

It's easy to get swept up by paint colors and staging. Stay grounded by evaluating the things that are expensive or impossible to change: location, school district, lot size, structural condition, and proximity to work. Cosmetic issues are cheap; foundation issues are not.

## Step 7: Make an Offer and Negotiate

Your agent will help you determine a competitive offer based on comparable sales. Depending on the market, you may be negotiating on price, closing costs, repairs, or closing timeline. Once your offer is accepted, you'll put down an earnest money deposit, which shows good faith and is later applied to your closing costs.

## Step 8: Home Inspection and Appraisal

A professional inspection (separate from the lender's appraisal) protects you from buying a home with hidden problems — roof issues, electrical hazards, plumbing leaks, or pest damage. The appraisal, ordered by your lender, confirms the home is worth what you're paying. If the appraisal comes in low, you may need to renegotiate or bring extra cash to closing.

## Step 9: Finalize Your Loan

After your offer is accepted, your lender will request final documentation — pay stubs, bank statements, tax returns — to move from pre-approval to final loan approval ("clear to close"). Avoid opening new credit accounts or making large purchases during this period; it can jeopardize your approval.

## Step 10: Closing Day

At closing, you'll sign a stack of documents, pay your remaining closing costs and down payment, and officially receive the keys. Before this day, request your Closing Disclosure (required by law at least three business days before closing) and compare it against your original Loan Estimate to catch any unexpected fees.

## A Final Word

The home buying process has a lot of moving parts, but breaking it into these ten steps makes it manageable. Before you start shopping, spend 20 minutes with our [Mortgage Calculator](/mortgage-calculator/) and [Affordability Calculator](/affordability-calculator/) — having real numbers in hand from day one will save you time, stress, and money throughout the entire process.

For official consumer protections during the home buying process, the [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/) offers free, unbiased resources worth bookmarking.

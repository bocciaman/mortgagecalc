If you've ever looked at your mortgage statement a few years into your loan and felt surprised by how little your balance has dropped, you've encountered amortization firsthand. It's one of the least intuitive parts of how mortgages work — and understanding it can change how you think about extra payments, refinancing, and even how long you plan to stay in a home.

## What Amortization Means

Amortization is the process of paying off a loan through regular, fixed payments over time, where each payment is split between **principal** (reducing what you owe) and **interest** (the cost of borrowing). The key detail most people don't realize: this split isn't constant. It shifts dramatically over the life of the loan.

## Why Early Payments Are Mostly Interest

Interest is calculated on your **remaining loan balance** each month. Early in your mortgage, your balance is at its highest point — so the interest portion of your payment is also at its highest. As the balance slowly decreases, less of each payment goes to interest and more goes to principal.

Here's a simplified look at a $320,000 loan at 7% over 30 years:

| Payment Year | Principal Portion | Interest Portion |
|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | ~$3,400 | ~$22,150 |
| Year 15 | ~$11,900 | ~$13,650 |
| Year 30 | ~$24,600 | ~$950 |

In year one, over 86% of your annual payments go toward interest. By year 30, that flips almost completely — over 96% goes to principal. This is why paying off a mortgage early in the loan term has a much bigger impact on total interest savings than paying extra in the later years. ## Why This Matters When Comparing Loan Terms

This is also the core mechanic behind why [15-year mortgages save so much more in total interest](/articles/30-year-vs-15-year-mortgage/) than 30-year mortgages, beyond just the shorter timeframe. Because principal is paid down faster in a 15-year loan, the balance — and therefore the interest charged on it — drops more quickly throughout the loan.

## Why This Matters for Selling or Refinancing

If you sell or refinance your home in the first several years of a 30-year mortgage, you'll likely be surprised by how little equity you've built through payments alone (separate from any appreciation in home value). This is an important consideration if you're not planning to stay in a home long-term — your "equity from paying down the loan" will be modest in years one through five, regardless of your monthly payment amount.

This also matters when deciding whether to refinance. If you're several years into your current loan and refinance into a new 30-year term, you effectively restart the amortization schedule — meaning you'll spend the early years of the new loan paying mostly interest again, even though you'd already moved past that phase on your original loan. We cover this tradeoff in [When Should You Refinance](/articles/when-should-you-refinance/).

## The Power of Extra Principal Payments

Because interest is calculated on your current balance, any extra payment toward principal — even a small one — reduces the balance that future interest is calculated against, for every remaining month of the loan. This compounding effect means even modest extra payments early in the loan can meaningfully shorten your payoff timeline and reduce total interest paid.

For example, an extra $200/month toward principal on the $320,000/7% loan above could shave nearly 6 years off a 30-year term and save tens of thousands in interest — without refinancing or changing your rate. ## The Bottom Line

Amortization explains one of the most counterintuitive parts of homeownership: your balance drops slowly at first, then increasingly quickly later in the loan. Understanding this curve — rather than assuming your balance drops at a steady, even pace — helps you make smarter decisions about extra payments, refinancing timing, and how long you plan to stay in a home.
